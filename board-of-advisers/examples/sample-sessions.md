## Example 1: Cross-Functional Strategy Session

### Scenario
SaaS company considering expansion to enterprise market while currently focused on SMB.

### Consultation 1: Strategic/CEO Adviser

**Founder Question**:
```
SITUATION: We're a project management SaaS at $50K MRR, growing 15% monthly.
90 SMB customers at $500-1K/month. Founder-led sales + one SDR.

CHALLENGE: Getting inbound interest from enterprise prospects (1,000+ employees)
but our product and sales process are built for SMB.

QUESTION: Should we pursue enterprise now, wait, or say no? If yes, how do we
approach it without derailing SMB growth?

CONTEXT:
- Have 18 months runway at current burn
- Product needs significant work for enterprise (SSO, advanced permissions, etc.)
- I (founder) have enterprise sales experience but SDR doesn't
- Could be 3-4 enterprise deals worth $10-15K/month each
```

**Strategic Adviser Response**:
"This is a classic growth crossroads. You have three viable paths:

**Path 1: Enterprise Now (Opportunistic)**
- Manually close 2-3 enterprise deals without building enterprise features
- Use as validation and learning
- Delay product investment until you prove enterprise demand
- Keep SMB as primary focus

**Path 2: SMB First, Enterprise Later (Sequential)**
- Focus 100% on SMB for next 6-12 months
- Use that time to solidify product, team, and processes
- Attack enterprise from position of strength later

**Path 3: Dual Track (Parallel)**
- Split focus between SMB and enterprise
- Risk: Resource dilution
- Only viable if you can hire/fund both tracks

**My Recommendation: Path 1 (Opportunistic Enterprise)**

Why:
- You have inbound demand (strongest signal)
- Your experience de-risks enterprise sales
- 2-3 deals would add 40-60% to MRR
- Learning is valuable even if you decide against enterprise later

**How to Execute**:
1. You personally handle enterprise deals
2. Close 3 enterprise customers manually (brute force, no fancy features)
3. If they renew and expand after 6 months → real opportunity
4. If they churn or don't expand → not ready yet
5. Keep SDR focused 100% on SMB

**Decision Criteria** (6 months from now):
- Are enterprise customers healthier than SMB? (retention, expansion)
- Can we articulate clear enterprise value prop?
- Are there 50+ similar enterprise prospects?
- Can we afford to build required features?

If yes to all four → build enterprise motion
If no → double down on SMB"

### Consultation 2: Finance Adviser

**Founder Question**:
"Based on the strategic advice to pursue 2-3 enterprise deals opportunistically, what are the financial implications? Can we afford this?"

**Finance Adviser Response**:
"Let's model this out:

**Current State**:
- MRR: $50K ($600K ARR)
- Growth: 15% monthly
- Runway: 18 months
- Burn: ~$33K/month (implied)

**Enterprise Deal Economics**:
- Target: 3 enterprise customers at $10-15K/month
- Timeline: 6 months to close 3 deals
- Additional cost: Your time (opportunity cost, not cash)

**Financial Impact**:

Best Case (3 deals at $15K/month):
- Additional MRR: $45K
- New total: $95K MRR
- Reduces burn by: $45K/month
- New runway: 2+ years (functionally infinite)
- **Risk mitigation score: HIGH** ✅

Realistic Case (3 deals at $10K/month):
- Additional MRR: $30K
- New total: $80K MRR
- Reduces burn by: $30K/month
- Improves runway significantly
- **Worth pursuing** ✅

Worst Case (only 1 deal at $10K/month):
- Additional MRR: $10K
- New total: $60K MRR
- Still helpful, reduces burn
- Haven't compromised SMB growth
- **Still a win** ✅

**Financial Risk Assessment**:
- CASH RISK: **LOW** (no major upfront investment)
- OPPORTUNITY COST: **MEDIUM** (your time away from SMB)
- PAYOFF POTENTIAL: **HIGH** (40-60% MRR increase possible)

**My Recommendation: Financially Sound**

The risk-reward is excellent because:
1. No major cash investment required
2. Meaningful upside to runway/growth
3. Downside is limited (your time for 6 months)
4. Can abort if not working by month 3

**Financial Guardrails**:
- Don't hire enterprise sales rep until you close 2 deals
- Don't build enterprise features until 3 deals signed
- Keep SMB growth above 10% monthly (if it dips below, refocus)
- Set monthly burn limit: Don't exceed $35K/month"

### Consultation 3: Product Adviser

**Founder Question**:
"If we're pursuing 2-3 enterprise deals without building enterprise features, how do we handle the product gaps? What's the minimum we need?"

**Product Adviser Response**:
[Full response detailing MVE (Minimum Viable Enterprise) approach, manual workarounds, etc.]

### Result

By consulting three advisers sequentially, the founder has:
- ✅ Strategic clarity on approach (opportunistic enterprise)
- ✅ Financial validation and guardrails
- ✅ Product roadmap that balances both markets
- ✅ Clear decision criteria for 6 months from now

## Example 2: Urgent Cash Flow Crisis

### Scenario
Service business facing cash crunch in 45 days.

**Founder Question to Finance Adviser**:
```
SITUATION: Service business (marketing agency), $40K monthly revenue,
6 employees + myself, payroll is $35K/month.

CHALLENGE: Just lost biggest client ($15K/month, 37.5% of revenue).
Effective in 30 days. Have $50K in bank. Will run out in ~45 days.

QUESTION: What do I do? Need immediate action plan.

CONTEXT:
- Pipeline: 3 prospects at proposal stage ($5-8K/month each)
- Have 2 small clients we could upsell
- Current clients: 5 total, ranging $3-8K/month
- Team: 2 senior ($75K salary), 4 junior ($45K salary)
- Equipment/overhead: $5K/month
```

**Finance Adviser Response**:

"This is a cash flow emergency. You have 45 days before crisis becomes catastrophic. Here's your triage plan:

**IMMEDIATE ACTIONS** (This Week):

**Day 1-2: Honest Assessment**
1. Recalculate runway precisely:
   - Cash: $50K
   - Burn: $40K/month (payroll + overhead)
   - Runway: 30-45 days until bank account hits zero
   - **Crisis point: Day 30** (need to make payroll)

2. Revenue projection:
   - Lost: -$15K (in 30 days)
   - Current: $25K/month remaining
   - New burn: **$15K/month negative** (spending $40K, earning $25K)
   - **You're losing $15K/month starting in 30 days**

**Day 3-5: Stop the Bleeding**

**OPTION A: Reduce Costs** (Least preferred, but fastest)
- Furlough 2 junior employees (saves $7.5K/month)
- Cut overhead 50% (saves $2.5K/month)
- Total savings: $10K/month
- New burn: -$5K/month (still negative but manageable for 10 months)

**OPTION B: Increase Revenue** (Preferred, but harder)
- Close 2 of 3 pipeline deals = $10-16K/month
- Upsell 2 current clients = $4-8K/month
- Total increase: $14-24K/month
- New position: Break-even or profitable

**OPTION C: Hybrid** (Most realistic)
- Close 1 pipeline deal = $5-8K/month
- Upsell 1 client = $2-4K/month
- Furlough 1 junior employee = $3.75K/month
- Total improvement: $10.75-15.75K/month
- New position: Near break-even

**MY RECOMMENDATION: Option C + Aggressive Revenue Push**

**WEEK 1 ACTIONS**:

**Monday**:
- Call all 3 pipeline prospects: 'We have availability opening up in 2 weeks. Can we accelerate our start date?'
- Goal: Close 1 this week with fast start

**Tuesday**:
- Call your 2 largest current clients: 'We have capacity opening up. Here's how we could expand our work together...'
- Goal: $2-4K monthly increase

**Wednesday**:
- Review team productivity: Who's billable 80%+ vs. 50%?
- Identify your least efficient employee (sadly, this might mean a layoff)

**Thursday**:
- Have honest conversation with team: 'We lost a big client. I'm fighting hard to replace the revenue, but we may need to make tough decisions in 2 weeks.'
- This primes team for possible furlough/layoff

**Friday**:
- Make decision: Are you closing deals?
- If yes → Keep team
- If no → Furlough/lay off to extend runway

**WEEKS 2-4: Execute Survival Plan**

**Revenue Actions**:
- Follow up daily with prospects
- Offer 20% discount for 'start this month': Costs you $1-1.5K but solves crisis
- Ask current clients for referrals: 'We have bandwidth for 1-2 clients. Who should we talk to?'
- Reach out to past clients: 'We have availability...'

**Cost Management**:
- Pause all non-essential expenses
- Renegotiate any possible contracts (software, rent)
- If no revenue materializes by Day 21 → Execute layoffs

**DECISION TREE**:

```
Day 7 Assessment:
├─ If closed 1 deal + 1 upsell → Continue with full team
├─ If closed 1 deal → Furlough 1 employee
└─ If closed 0 deals → Furlough 2 employees + aggressive cost cuts

Day 21 Assessment:
├─ If at break-even → Continue plan
├─ If burning $5K+/month → More cuts needed
└─ If burning $10K+/month → Consider shutting down gracefully
```

**LAST RESORT OPTIONS** (If revenue doesn't materialize):

1. **Small Business Loan**: $25-50K to extend runway (explore immediately)
2. **Equity Partner**: Sell 20-30% for cash injection
3. **Acqui-hire**: Sell team and client base to larger agency
4. **Wind Down Gracefully**: Before completely out of cash

**METRICS TO TRACK DAILY**:
- Cash balance
- Days of runway remaining
- Pipeline progress (calls, proposals, closes)
- New leads generated

**The Hard Truth**:
You're in survival mode. You need to close deals or cut costs—ideally both. Start today. Every day counts when you have 45 days of runway.

Call me back next Monday with update. We'll adjust the plan based on results."

### Result

Clear 45-day survival plan with:
- ✅ Daily/weekly action items
- ✅ Decision points at Day 7 and Day 21
- ✅ Multiple scenarios planned
- ✅ Hard numbers and timelines
- ✅ Last resort options identified

## Example 3: Product Roadmap Decision

### Scenario
Product team divided on whether to build AI features or fix core platform issues.

**Founder Question to Product/Industry Adviser**:

```
SITUATION: B2B workflow automation tool, 200 customers, $50K MRR, 8-person team

CHALLENGE: Product team is split:
- Engineers want to fix technical debt and core reliability
- Product manager wants to build AI features (competitors are shipping AI)
- Sales says customers keep asking about AI

QUESTION: What should we prioritize?

CONTEXT:
- Current issues:
  - Platform crashes 2-3x/month (5-20 minute outages)
  - Mobile experience is poor
  - Integrations are fragile
  - Reporting is basic

- AI opportunities:
  - AI-suggested workflows
  - Predictive analytics
  - Natural language automation creation

- Competitive landscape:
  - 2 of 3 main competitors launched AI features in last quarter
  - Customer win rate has dropped from 30% to 22% in last quarter

- Resources:
  - Can only do one major initiative per quarter
  - Have 1 ML-capable engineer, 3 fullstack engineers
```

**Product/Industry Adviser Response**:

"You're facing a classic 'innovation vs. reliability' dilemma, made urgent by competitive pressure. Let me break this down:

**THE REAL QUESTION**:
This isn't 'AI vs. core platform.' It's: 'Why is our win rate dropping, and what will fix it?'

**HYPOTHESIS TESTING**:

First, let's validate what's actually happening:

**Call 5 lost deals from the last quarter and ask**:
1. 'What was the primary reason you chose [competitor]?'
2. 'Did AI features factor into your decision?'
3. 'What concerns did you have about our product?'

**Likely Scenarios**:

**Scenario A**: 'We chose [competitor] because they have AI features.'
→ You have an AI problem
→ Build AI features next quarter

**Scenario B**: 'We loved your product but worried about reliability after the demo failed.'
→ You have a reliability problem
→ Fix core platform next quarter

**Scenario C**: 'They had features X, Y, Z that you don't have' (not AI)
→ You have a feature gap problem
→ Neither AI nor reliability is the priority

**MY PREDICTION** (based on 15 years in B2B SaaS):

You'll find it's **Scenario B** (reliability), not Scenario A (AI).

Here's why:
- 2-3 crashes per month is a HUGE red flag for buyers
- Poor mobile + fragile integrations signal immaturity
- Win rate dropped 27% (30→22%) = systematic problem
- If it were AI, win rate would drop slowly, not suddenly

**AI is visible in demos, but reliability kills deals in evaluation.**

**MY RECOMMENDATION**:

**Q1: Stability Sprint** (Next 90 Days)
1. **Zero crashes goal**: Fix underlying reliability issues
2. **Integration hardening**: Make top 5 integrations bulletproof
3. **Mobile baseline**: Bring mobile to functional (not great, just usable)
4. **Quick AI win**: Add ONE simple AI feature that shows you're paying attention
   - Example: AI-suggested automation templates (1-2 week effort)
   - This is 'AI table stakes' to not look behind

**Q2: AI Differentiation** (Following 90 Days)
5. **After** stability is proven, go deep on AI features
6. You'll build from a position of strength
7. Won't lose deals due to crashes while building AI

**THE PRINCIPLE**:
'Innovate from stability, not instead of stability.'

Customers say they want AI. But they buy reliability. A demo that crashes loses to a boring competitor that works.

**TACTICAL ROADMAP**:

**Month 1**:
- Root cause analysis of crashes → fixes
- Start basic AI templates feature
- Harden top 3 integrations

**Month 2**:
- Implement crash fixes
- Launch AI templates (quick win for sales)
- Fix mobile core workflows

**Month 3**:
- Monitor: Zero crashes for 30 days
- Measure: Win rate recovery
- Prepare: Deep AI features for Q2

**Month 4+**:
- NOW build sophisticated AI features
- You have stable platform + AI story

**COMMUNICATION PLAN**:

**To Engineers**:
'We're prioritizing stability first because it's losing us deals. AI comes immediately after. Both are important, but stability is urgent.'

**To Product Manager**:
'We're doing AI—just strategically. Quick win in 2 weeks (templates), deep features in Q2. But we can't build AI on a shaky foundation.'

**To Sales**:
'You'll have an AI feature to demo in 2 weeks. The full AI roadmap comes in Q2. Meanwhile, we're fixing the reliability issues that are actually killing deals.'

**VALIDATION PLAN**:

Track these metrics weekly:
- **Uptime %**: Goal: 99.9% (currently ~98.5%)
- **Integration errors**: Goal: <1% error rate
- **Win rate**: Should recover to 28-30% within 60 days if this is right
- **Lost deal reasons**: AI should drop as a reason

If win rate doesn't recover after stability improvements → revisit assumption

**The Hard Truth**:
Your competitors' AI features are getting attention, but I'd bet your reliability issues are losing more deals. Fix the foundation first, then build the future.

Make those 5 customer calls this week. Let me know what you learn."

### Result

Clear path forward with:
- ✅ Hypothesis to validate first (customer calls)
- ✅ Prioritized roadmap based on likely outcome
- ✅ Quick win AI feature to address perception
- ✅ Long-term plan that addresses both concerns
- ✅ Communication strategy for team alignment
- ✅ Metrics to validate approach

## Example 4: Scaling Sales Team

### Scenario
Founder ready to hire first sales rep but uncertain about approach.

**Founder Question to Sales Adviser**:

```
SITUATION: B2B SaaS, $30K MRR, been doing all sales myself for 18 months

CHALLENGE: I can't keep up with inbound leads (20-30/month) while also
running the company. Need to hire sales help.

QUESTION: Should I hire a junior SDR, experienced AE, or sales leader?
What should I pay? How do I know if they're good?

CONTEXT:
- Current sales process:
  - Inbound leads fill out form
  - I do discovery call (30 min)
  - I do demo (45 min)
  - I send proposal
  - I close (usually 1-2 follow-ups)
  - Cycle: 14-21 days
  - Win rate: 35%
  - Deal size: $400-1,000/month

- My sales capacity:
  - Can handle 8-10 deals/month
  - Getting 20-30 qualified leads/month
  - Losing 10-20 deals due to capacity

- Budget:
  - Can afford $100-120K total comp
  - Prefer to start conservative
```

**Sales Adviser Response**:

"Great problem to have—too many leads! But hiring your first sales rep is critical. Get it wrong and you'll waste 6 months and $50K. Let's get it right.

**THE BRUTAL TRUTH FIRST**:

Most first sales hires fail because:
1. Founder hires too junior (can't close without training)
2. Founder hires too senior (costs too much, gets bored)
3. Founder doesn't transfer knowledge effectively
4. Founder picks wrong person for the sales motion

**YOUR SITUATION ANALYSIS**:

You have a **founder-led sales motion that works**:
- ✅ 35% win rate (great)
- ✅ 14-21 day cycle (fast)
- ✅ Process is documented (hopefully!)
- ✅ Inbound leads (easier to sell)
- ✅ Deal size $400-1K (good unit economics)

**This is IDEAL for first sales hire.**

**WHO TO HIRE: Experienced SDR or Junior AE**

**NOT**:
- ❌ Sales Leader/VP (too senior, wrong stage)
- ❌ Enterprise AE (wrong sales motion)
- ❌ Fresh grad (too junior for full-cycle)

**YES**:
- ✅ **Option 1**: SDR with 2-3 years experience who wants to move into closing
  - Can handle first call + demo
  - You close until they prove themselves
  - Lower risk ($60-70K OTE)

- ✅ **Option 2**: Junior AE with 1-2 years closing experience (RECOMMENDED)
  - Can handle full cycle
  - Has closed 20-30 deals before
  - Hungry to prove themselves ($80-100K OTE)

**MY RECOMMENDATION: Junior AE (Option 2)**

Why:
- Your cycle is short enough one person can manage full cycle
- Splitting into SDR + closer is premature at this stage
- Junior AE can close while you focus on company
- If they're good, they can scale with you

**THE PROFILE**:

**Must-Haves**:
- 1-3 years B2B sales experience
- Closed at least 20 deals in last 12 months
- Experience with $400-5K deal sizes
- Comfortable with consultative selling (not pushy)
- Self-motivated (will work with minimal supervision)

**Nice-to-Haves**:
- SaaS experience
- Used CRM systems (HubSpot, Salesforce)
- Industry knowledge
- Experience with your customer type

**Red Flags**:
- 🚩 Changed jobs 3+ times in 3 years
- 🚩 Never achieved quota
- 🚩 Only worked at huge companies (won't handle startup chaos)
- 🚩 Can't explain sales process clearly

**COMPENSATION STRUCTURE**:

**Base Salary**: $50-60K
**Variable/Commission**: $30-40K at quota
**OTE (On-Target Earnings)**: $80-100K
**Quota**: $320-400K annually ($25-33K monthly)

**Commission Structure**:
- 10-12% of Annual Contract Value (ACV)
- Example: $500/month deal = $6,000 ACV = $600-720 commission

**Ramp Period**:
- Month 1: Training (50% commission, no quota)
- Month 2: Shadow + first deals (75% commission, 50% quota)
- Month 3: Full commission, 75% quota
- Month 4+: Full quota

**THE HIRING PROCESS**:

**Phase 1: Screening** (Week 1)
- Post job to AngelList, LinkedIn, sales-specific boards
- Screen resumes for experience markers
- 15-min phone screen (energy, communication, basic fit)
- Select top 5 candidates

**Phase 2: Interviews** (Week 2-3)

Interview 1 (You, 45 min):
- Ask about their sales process
- 'Walk me through your last 3 closed deals'
- 'Tell me about a deal you lost and why'
- 'How do you qualify leads?'
- Look for: Process, accountability, learning mentality

Interview 2 (Role-Play, 30 min):
- 'Pretend I'm a prospect. Sell me your last product.'
- Evaluate: Questioning, listening, value proposition
- This is CRITICAL - shows actual skill

Interview 3 (References):
- Call 2-3 references (must include sales manager)
- Ask: 'What was their quota and what % did they hit?'
- Ask: 'Would you hire them again?'

**Phase 3: Offer** (Week 4)
- Extend offer to top candidate
- Include: Clear quota, commission structure, expectations
- Get written acceptance

**ONBOARDING PLAN** (First 30 Days):

**Week 1: Learn the Product**
- Become a user themselves
- Study all product materials
- Shadow customer success calls
- Take fake product demo from you

**Week 2: Learn the Customer**
- Read 10 customer case studies
- Listen to 10 recorded sales calls
- Talk to 5 current customers
- Understand customer pain points

**Week 3: Learn the Process**
- Shadow your sales calls (5-10)
- Learn CRM and tools
- Review proposal templates
- Understand pricing and negotiation boundaries

**Week 4: Start Selling (with Training Wheels)**
- Lead discovery calls (you listen)
- You lead demos (they assist)
- They send proposals (you review)
- Goal: 2-3 deals closed with heavy support

**SUCCESS METRICS**:

**Month 1-3** (Ramp Period):
- Completed training milestones
- 2-3 closed deals
- Positive customer feedback
- Good pipeline management

**Month 4-6** (Proving Period):
- 60-80% quota attainment ($15-20K MRR)
- 25%+ win rate (lower than you is ok at first)
- Self-sufficient on most deals

**Month 7+** (Ramped):
- 80-100% quota attainment
- 30%+ win rate
- Can handle full cycle independently

**RED FLAGS TO WATCH**:
- Win rate below 20% after Month 3
- Not learning from losses
- Poor CRM hygiene
- Blaming leads/product/pricing
- Not asking for help

**If they're not hitting 80% quota by Month 6, they're probably not going to make it.**

**YOUR ROLE**:

**Months 1-3**: Heavy involvement (10-15 hours/week)
- Training and shadowing
- Review every deal
- Weekly coaching sessions

**Months 4-6**: Moderate involvement (5-8 hours/week)
- Weekly pipeline review
- Help with stuck deals
- Celebrate wins

**Months 7+**: Light involvement (2-3 hours/week)
- Monthly check-ins
- Help with major deals
- Strategic coaching

**DOCUMENTATION NEEDED** (Before Hiring):

Create these now:
1. **Sales Playbook**: Your process documented
2. **Call Scripts**: Discovery questions, demo flow
3. **Objection Handling**: Common objections + responses
4. **Pricing/Discounting Policy**: Clear boundaries
5. **CRM Process**: How to track everything

If you haven't documented your process, do that THIS WEEK. You can't transfer what isn't documented.

**FINAL ADVICE**:

The first sales hire is a multiplier. A great hire will 2-3x your sales capacity. A bad hire will drain time and money.

**Take your time finding the right person. It's better to wait an extra month for a great fit than rush and hire wrong.**

Budget 3 months to find, hire, and ramp them. But when you find the right person, you'll know—and your business will accelerate.

Want me to review your job description and interview questions once you draft them?"

### Result

Comprehensive first sales hire plan:
- ✅ Clear role definition (Junior AE)
- ✅ Specific compensation structure
- ✅ Detailed hiring process
- ✅ 90-day onboarding plan
- ✅ Success metrics and red flags
- ✅ Documentation checklist

## Key Takeaways from These Examples

### 1. **Context is King**
Every effective consultation includes:
- Current state (numbers, facts)
- Specific challenge (not vague)
- Clear question (what decision needs to be made)
- Relevant constraints (time, money, resources)

### 2. **Sequential Consultation**
Complex decisions benefit from multiple advisers:
- Start with strategic perspective
- Add functional expertise
- Synthesize into action plan

### 3. **Advisers Give Frameworks, Not Just Answers**
Notice how advisers:
- Provide decision-making frameworks
- Teach underlying principles
- Give tools for future similar decisions

### 4. **Immediate Next Steps**
Good advisory sessions end with:
- Clear action items
- Specific timelines
- Measurable outcomes
- Follow-up plan

### 5. **Honest Assessment**
Advisers are most valuable when they:
- Challenge your assumptions
- Point out uncomfortable truths
- Identify risks you haven't considered
- Provide realistic timelines and expectations
